#2
Round 1, Pick #2 Overall
ARVELL
REESE
EDGE — Ohio State — 6'4", 255 lbs — Junior

This is the pick. Since the Jermaine Johnson-for-T'Vondre Sweat trade, the football world has connected the dots. You don't trade your starting edge rusher for a nose tackle unless you're planning to replace him with an upgrade. Arvell Reese is that upgrade.

Reese is the most complete defensive player in this draft class. At Ohio State, he was an every-down weapon — rushing the passer with violent hands and a devastating speed-to-power move, stuffing the run with anchor strength that belies his 255-pound frame, and dropping into coverage with surprising fluidity for a player his size.

"Reese is the best pass rusher I've evaluated since the Bosa brothers. The burst off the edge, the plan, the counter — it's elite."
— Daniel Jeremiah, NFL Network (#3 on DJ's Top 50 v2.0)

In Aaron Glenn's 3-4 defense, Reese becomes the bookend opposite Will McDonald IV (21.5 career sacks). With Odafe Oweh signed as a third edge rusher and Bradley Chubb as a veteran rotation piece, the Jets suddenly go from one of the worst pass rushes in football to one of the deepest. Reese on early downs, McDonald on passing downs, Oweh as the chaos element, Chubb as the closer. That's a war room.

Fernando Mendoza (QB, Indiana) goes #1 to the Raiders. The Jets are on the clock. This pick has been locked since November.

#16
Round 1, Pick #16 Overall — via Indianapolis
MANSOOR
DELANE
Cornerback — LSU — 6'2", 190 lbs — Junior

This is the most critical pick of the entire draft. The Jets have no CB1. Sauce is in Indianapolis. D.J. Reed is in Detroit. Michael Carter II is in Philadelphia. The cupboard is bare — Ja'Sir Taylor and Kool-Aid McKinstry (coming off IR) are the only corners under contract. You cannot field a defense in 2026 without a Day 1 starter at corner.

Mansoor Delane is the best cornerback in this class. The LSU product is a 6'2" press-man corner with elite ball skills — 14 passes defended and 4 interceptions in 2025. He earned first-team All-American honors and was the highest-graded SEC corner on PFF. His length, physicality at the line, and ability to mirror routes make him an ideal fit for Glenn's scheme, which demands aggressive, physical play at the perimeter.

With Sauce gone, this isn't a luxury pick. It's a survival pick. The Jets cannot enter 2026 without a first-round corner.

The risk: Delane is projected in the top 10-15 range on most boards. If he's there at #16, you sprint to the podium. Daniel Jeremiah has him at #8 overall. He could be gone. If he is, the fallback plan matters — and there are options (see below).

Glenn's Detroit defense ran heavy man coverage with corners who could play physical at the line and hold up in isolation. Delane is that prototype. He's not replacing Sauce — nobody is — but he's the closest thing this class has to a franchise corner.

// TRADE OPTIONS

TRADE BACK
FROM #16

Pick #2 is locked. Everything else is on the table. The Jets have 6 picks in a draft where they need to fill 8+ roster holes. Trading back from #16 is a real option — young bodies on cheap rookie contracts is how you rebuild a gutted roster. Here are three scenarios worth exploring.

Scenario A — Small Slide
#16 → #22 + #87
Trade partner: Atlanta Falcons
ATL needs a franchise LT to protect Michael Penix. If Kelvin Banks or another tackle is there at #16 and sliding, Atlanta jumps to grab him. Jets move back 6 spots and pick up a 3rd-round pick.
At #22, the Jets can still target Colton Hood (CB, Tennessee) or Jordyn Tyson (WR, Arizona State) — both projected in the 15-25 range. Add pick #87 and you've got an extra Day 2 dart throw at CB depth or a developmental DL.
SEND #16 GET #22 GET #87 (RD 3)
DRAFT CHART: ~1,000 pts → ~780 + ~220 pts // Fair value
Scenario B — Drop to Late 1st
#16 → #28 + #60
Trade partner: Denver Broncos
Denver is building around Bo Nix and needs a weapon — if a top WR or edge is sliding, they leap from #28 to #16. The Jets pick up a 2nd-round pick on top of sliding back 12 spots.
At #28, the Jets can still find a starter — Darien Porter (CB, Iowa State) or Benjamin Morrison (CB, Notre Dame) could be Day 1 contributors. Pick #60 targets IOL or WR depth. This is the sweet spot — you still get a first-rounder's 5th-year option AND extra Day 2 capital.
SEND #16 GET #28 GET #60 (RD 2)
DRAFT CHART: ~1,000 pts → ~660 + ~350 pts // Slight overpay by DEN
Scenario C — Maximum Capital
#16 → #34 + #42 + #103
Trade partner: Houston Texans
Houston is in win-now mode with Stroud. They need immediate help and jump into the first round to grab a falling blue-chip player. The Jets cash out #16 entirely for three Day 2 picks.
This is the high-volume play. Three extra picks means 3 more young starters on 4-year rookie deals. Target CB at #34, IOL at #42, WR/DL at #103. The Jets go from 6 draft picks to 8 picks in the top 103. That's how you rebuild a roster in one offseason.
SEND #16 GET #34 (RD 2) GET #42 (RD 2) GET #103 (RD 4)
DRAFT CHART: ~1,000 pts → ~560 + ~480 + ~60 pts // Jets win the math
The math is simple: this roster has too many holes for 6 picks. Every trade back from #16 gives you another cheap starter on a 4-year rookie deal. You're not drafting for next year — you're drafting for 2027 and beyond.
R2
Round 2, Pick ~40 — via Dallas (Q. Williams Trade)
OLAIVAVEGA
IOANE
Interior Offensive Line — Penn State — 6'4", 315 lbs — Senior

The ideal scenario: Delane at #16, Ioane here at ~40. Ioane is DJ's #11 overall prospect, but guards historically slide in the draft — positional premium pushes them down. If the board breaks right, a franchise guard falls into your lap at a second-round price. That's the bet.

Olaivavega Ioane is the best interior offensive lineman in the 2026 class. The Penn State guard allowed zero QB hits in 2025 — zero — while grading out as PFF's top-rated run-blocking guard in the Big Ten. He's a mauler in the run game and a wall in pass protection. The kind of guard who makes a line identity.

Fashanu at LT. Ioane at LG. Tippmann at C. AVT at RG. Membou at RT. That's a line that can actually protect Malik Willis and run the ball. That's a line with an identity.

The Jets lost John Simpson (UFA) and have a gaping hole at left guard. Ioane fills it immediately. Daniel Jeremiah has him at #11 overall — getting him at ~40 would be a steal, but his lack of positional premium (guard vs. tackle) could push him down boards. The Combine will be key — if he tests as a top-15 athlete among guards, he won't last.

In a draft where the Jets need to rebuild an entire secondary AND an offensive line, getting a franchise guard with the Dallas pick is the kind of value that changes a rebuild. The Q. Williams trade gave them this pick. Make it count.
