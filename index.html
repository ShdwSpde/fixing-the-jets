THE TEARDOWN

This isn’t a story about one bad season. It’s about a franchise that burned itself to the studs to rebuild the right way. Over the last two years, the Jets shed every marquee name on the roster — willingly, aggressively, and with a plan. Aaron Rodgers. Sauce Gardner. Quinnen Williams. Davante Adams. C.J. Mosley. D.J. Reed. The entire identity of the team walked out the door.

The defining moment came on a single day:

November 4, 2025

The Jets traded Sauce Gardner — the Defensive Rookie of the Year, two-time first-team All-Pro, and the best cornerback in football — to Indianapolis. Hours later, they shipped Quinnen Williams — three-time Pro Bowler, the No. 3 overall pick from 2019 — to Dallas. Two franchise cornerstones. Same day. The signal was unmistakable: this is a reload, not a repair.

GLENN'S DEFENSE: THE SCHEME

Hybrid front. Even-front, one-gap principles that can flex into 3-4 looks. Versatile, athletic defenders who play multiple roles. Think Detroit — aggressive, multiple, built on the front seven. With Sweat at nose tackle and Mazi Smith as the incumbent DT, Glenn needs impact at edge, inside linebacker, and safety. He is calling his own defense.

DRAFT ASSUMPTION

Arvell Reese, EDGE, Ohio State at #2 overall. The freak athlete is one of the fastest edge rushers in the 2026 class. After trading Jermaine Johnson, this pick writes itself. Breece Hall will be franchise or transition tagged (~$12M). The rest — we address in free agency. The #16 pick and Day 2 capital will be covered in Part 2: The Draft Board.

THE YOUNG CORE ALREADY IN PLACE

The teardown wasn’t just subtraction. The Jets have assembled a young foundation: Garrett Wilson (WR1, $10M cap hit in year 1 of his extension), Will McDonald IV (EDGE, 21.5 career sacks, 5th-year option being picked up — the Jets’ best pass rusher), Jamien Sherwood (ILB, 154 tackles, locked in on $45M extension), Olu Fashanu (LT, dominant), Armand Membou (RT, All-Rookie — they formed the NFL’s 3rd-best tackle duo by pass-block grade over the final 12 weeks), Joe Tippmann (C, 24, played every snap in 2025 — extension candidate), Mason Taylor (TE, game-winner as a rookie), Adonai Mitchell (WR, vertical threat from the Sauce trade), T’Vondre Sweat (NT, 24, from the Johnson trade), Mazi Smith (DT, from the Q trade). The O-line started the same five all 17 games, all 27 or younger — though both guards (John Simpson and AVT) are now UFAs. Free agency fills the gaps around this core.

01
Move 01 — Go Get Your Quarterback

MALIK
WILLIS

QB | Age 26 | Green Bay Packers

This is the move. Forget the bridge quarterbacks. Forget another retread. The Jets have already tried that with Rodgers and Fields — combined cost: over $100M in dead money and cap commitments for a combined record of 6-25. Malik Willis is 26 years old, spent two years learning behind Jordan Love in one of the NFL’s best offensive systems, and is the most coveted QB in this free agent class.

Willis is mobile, has a cannon arm, and is young enough that this isn’t a rental — it’s the first real swing at a franchise quarterback since the Jets drafted Sam Darnold.

Multiple reports confirm the Jets will be “big players” for Willis. Green Bay’s Brian Gutekunst has publicly conceded they’ll likely lose him. The Jets should be first in line. Give Willis Garrett Wilson, Adonai Mitchell, Mason Taylor, Breece Hall, and an elite tackle duo in Fashanu and Membou. That’s a real supporting cast — something the Jets haven’t had around a QB in years.

WHY NOT COUSINS?

He’s 37. After burning $100M+ on veteran QBs who didn’t work, the answer is not another one. Kirk Cousins went 5-3 with Atlanta and threw 10 TDs — fine for a bridge. But this franchise needs to stop bridging and start building. Willis is the future.

02
Move 02 — Unleash the Pass Rush

ODAFE
OWEH

EDGE | Age 27 | Los Angeles Chargers

The Jets already have Will McDonald IV — 21.5 career sacks, 8 in 2025, 5th-year option being picked up. He’s a legitimate starter. But Glenn’s defense eats edge rushers. You can never have enough. With Reese at #2 and McDonald returning, adding Oweh gives you a three-headed monster that can rotate and stay fresh. Oweh exploded after his midseason trade from Baltimore to the Chargers: 7.5 sacks in 12 games. He’s long-armed, explosive, and at 27 he’s entering his prime.

In Glenn’s hybrid front, Oweh can stand up as a 3-4 OLB or put his hand in the dirt as a 4-3 DE. His versatility is exactly what this defense demands.

A rotation of Reese, McDonald, Oweh, and Chubb with Sweat and Mazi Smith clogging the interior — that’s a defensive front with pass-rush juice from every angle. The Chargers have ~$100M in cap space and may try to retain him — the Jets need to outbid aggressively. This is the kind of player you overpay for because the positional value is enormous.

WHY NOT HENDRICKSON?

Trey Hendrickson is 31 and coming off core muscle surgery that limited him to 4 sacks in 7 games. He got $29M from the Bengals last year. Why pay for the decline when you can pay for the ascent? Oweh is four years younger with a higher ceiling.

Move 03 — The Quarterback of the Defense

QUAY
WALKER

ILB | Age 25 | Green Bay Packers

The Jets have Jamien Sherwood locked in at $45M — 154 tackles in 2025, he’s not going anywhere ($25M dead cap). But Sherwood struggled in coverage and was benched for a stretch. He needs a partner who elevates the room, not another liability. With C.J. Mosley retired and Quincy Williams a UFA, that partner is Quay Walker — a four-time 100-tackle linebacker with 9 career sacks and 29 tackles for loss. He’s only 25. The Packers declined his 5th-year option worth $14.7M — their loss, Glenn’s gain.

This is the move nobody will talk about on television. It’s the one that makes the defense work.

Walker is the prototypical modern three-down ILB. He can blitz, he can cover, and he tackles sideline to sideline — the exact skill set Sherwood lacks in coverage. Walker + Sherwood gives you a run-stuffing thumper paired with a sideline-to-sideline playmaker. In a defense built around Reese, McDonald, and Oweh hunting the quarterback and Sweat clogging the middle, those two clean up everything in between. At 25, the Jets would be getting Walker’s best years — the exact timeline this rebuild demands.

Move 04 — Fix the Secondary

BRYAN
COOK

S | Age 26 | Kansas City Chiefs

The Jets lost everything in the secondary. Sauce Gardner — traded. D.J. Reed — signed with Detroit. Michael Carter II — traded. The safety room was already the worst unit on the team. The cupboard isn’t bare — it’s been demolished. Bryan Cook is the first piece of putting it back together.

Glenn’s defense needs a tone-setter in the secondary. A hitter. A communicator. Someone who’s been in the biggest moments football has to offer. Cook is all of that.

Cook is a 26-year-old Super Bowl champion from Kansas City who plays with the physicality and intelligence Glenn demands. He’s a box safety who can come downhill and punish the run but has improved in coverage every year. At ~$11M/yr, he’s a value signing at a position of desperate need. The secondary will need more — cornerback is a massive hole addressed in Part 2 — but Cook gives you the anchor to build around.

Move 05 — The Mougey Reunion + Protect Your QB

BRADLEY CHUBB
+ ALIJAH VERA-TUCKER

EDGE + OG | Age 30 / 25 | Released by MIA / NYJ Own FA

The final move is a two-part play. Bradley Chubb and Darren Mougey overlapped in Denver for four-plus years. Mougey was in the building when the Broncos drafted Chubb 5th overall in 2018, watched his 12-sack rookie season, saw his work ethic, his medicals, his recovery, his leadership. This is the kind of intel advantage GMs build careers on.

8.5 sacks in 2025 after missing all of 2024 with a torn ACL, meniscus, and patellar tendon. Won AFC Defensive Player of the Week in Week 17. He’s not done.

Miami released him to save $7.3M. At 30, on a 1-year prove-it deal, the risk is minimal — if he’s healthy, you’re getting an 8+ sack edge rusher for $8M rotating behind Reese and Oweh. Three-deep at edge is how Glenn wants to play.

Then re-sign Alijah Vera-Tucker at guard. With Fashanu and Membou locked in at tackle, AVT slots inside to protect Willis. He’s versatile, the Jets know him, and he’s a buy-low coming off a torn triceps. The offensive line: Fashanu–Need–Tippmann–AVT–Membou. Four of five locked in, all 27 or younger. Tippmann played all 1,045 snaps at center. AVT slides back to right guard where he started in 2025. Left guard — where John Simpson was an elite run blocker in 2024 before falling off — becomes a draft or value FA target.

THE HONEST ASSESSMENT — UPDATED

The roster is better on defense than this plan predicted — Minkah Fitzpatrick is a genuine upgrade over the Bryan Cook target, and adding David Onyemata gives the interior line real depth alongside Sweat and Mazi Smith. But CB1 is still the most critical hole on this roster. Nahshon Wright (1yr/$5.5M) is a rotational piece, not an answer. Sauce Gardner, D.J. Reed, and Michael Carter II all gone — the corner room remains borderline emergency level. The QB room is a deliberate one-year placeholder. Geno Smith is capable and experienced, but he is a bridge. Left guard got addressed with Dylan Parham. The draft now has to handle CB1, edge rotation depth, and — critically — a developmental quarterback who can be the answer in 2027.

WHERE THE JETS
DIVERGED

QB: Bridge Over Franchise

The plan bet on Malik Willis — 26, athletic, coming off two years in Green Bay's system — as the franchise cornerstone. The Jets said no. They traded for Geno Smith at 35, on a team-friendly 2026 deal, as an explicit bridge to the 2027 draft class. This isn't a team that believes in its 2026 QB options. It's a team positioning for next year.

LB: Veteran Infrastructure Over Youth

Quay Walker (25) was the rebuild pick — young, ascending, the exact age profile this plan demanded. The Jets chose Demario Davis at 37. Year 15 in the league. Two Super Bowl rings. The calculus shifted: with a bridge QB and a young core that needs teaching, you need veterans who know how to win, not young players learning alongside young players.

Safety: Upgraded the Target

Bryan Cook was the smart, cost-effective call. Minkah Fitzpatrick is just better — 81.8 PFF grade, legitimate star, traded for a seventh-round pick. The Jets didn't go cheap at safety; they found a deal. This is the one deviation from the plan that's clearly an upgrade in both quality and cost.

Edge: Oweh Priced Out, Ossai In

Oweh went to Washington for 4yr/$100M — a price the Jets apparently couldn't or wouldn't match. Joseph Ossai (5 sacks in each of the last two seasons) is a capable starter, but he's not the volatile upside play Oweh represented. With Reese presumably coming at #2, the edge room is Reese + McDonald + Ossai. Functional. Not terrifying.

Guard: Parham, Not AVT

Alijah Vera-Tucker — a Jet, known quantity, coming off a torn triceps — was the continuity call. The Jets signed Dylan Parham from the Raiders instead. Two-year deal at similar money. Parham is younger (26) and cheaper on the guarantee side. AVT's market clearly exceeded what the Jets offered.

The Overall Shift: 2027 Mode

The plan imagined a team betting on 2026. The actual Jets are betting on 2027 — bridge QB, veteran stopgaps at LB, a CB room that's still thin, and three first-round picks next year. They're building the roster to be competitive enough to develop young players while positioning for the next draft's quarterback class.

// How This Changes Draft Considerations at #2 and #16

At #2: Arvell Reese — still the call. The plan assumed Reese here, and that logic only strengthened after free agency. Oweh went to Washington. Ossai is a starter, not a difference-maker. Will McDonald IV is legitimately good but not a first-team threat. The Jets desperately need a true #1 pass rusher opposite McDonald — a player the entire defense is built around. Reese is still the betting favorite at -140 to go second overall, and nothing in free agency changes that calculus.

The QB question is now live, not closed. With Geno Smith explicitly positioned as a bridge, there is a genuine argument to take a developmental quarterback at #16 or to trade up from #16 if a prospect falls. Ty Simpson (Alabama) is the name appearing in Jets mock draft roundups. He doesn't need to start in 2026 — he needs to be ready for 2027 when Geno's bridge ends and three first-round picks worth of draft capital is available to reload around him.

The trade-down scenario is now real. With Geno as QB and the 2027 draft potentially loaded, there is a legitimate argument to sell #2 — add a fourth first-round pick in 2027 — and enter next year with an unprecedented war chest. The counterargument: edge rushers of Reese's profile don't come along every year, and the rest of the 2026 class thins out fast after him.

At #16: CB1 is now the undisputed priority. Nahshon Wright is signed but is not a number-one corner. The secondary has Fitzpatrick at safety but still no legitimate CB1 after losing Sauce, Reed, and Carter II. If a top cornerback falls to 16, that pick writes itself. Left guard (with Parham signed, less urgent) and edge depth are secondary considerations.
